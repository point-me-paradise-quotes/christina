Where You'll Go

Your Three Ports

Costa Maya
Quintana Roo, Mexico  ·  Wed, July 8  ·  8:00 AM – 6:00 PM (10 hrs)

Costa Maya sits at the southern end of Quintana Roo, far enough from Cancún that the organized tour traffic thins out considerably. The pier drops you into a purpose-built cruise zone — walk past it and you're standing at the edge of one of the largest barrier reef systems in the Western Hemisphere. Snorkeling here runs warm and clear, with elkhorn coral close enough to the surface that a 7-year-old with a mask and fins can see everything worth seeing without going deep. For a history stop, the ruins at Chacchoben sit inside jungle thick enough to block the midday July heat — smaller and far less trafficked than Tulum, with a guide who actually has time to talk.

Mahahual village, a 10-minute cab ride from the pier, is where local fishermen eat lunch. Open-air tables, fresh ceviche, cold beer, and almost no one from the ship. Ten hours in port gives you time to do all of it.

Isla de Roatan
Bay Islands, Honduras  ·  Thu, July 9  ·  8:00 AM – 4:00 PM (8 hrs)

Roatan is a narrow limestone ridge surrounded by the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef — the second-longest reef system in the world after Australia's. The water off the west end holds 60-foot visibility on an average day. Most cruise passengers push toward West Bay Beach, where the reef starts 30 yards from shore and the snorkeling is immediate, with no boat required. For something with more local character, West End village is a 10-minute ride: weathered wooden docks over turquoise water, dive shops that have been running the same walls for decades, and fish tacos at open tables above the reef.

The drive across the island ridge takes 20 minutes and passes through cloud forest. The view from the top — Caribbean on both sides — is worth including in the morning before the beach. Eight hours is enough to do both.

Cozumel
Quintana Roo, Mexico  ·  Fri, July 10  ·  8:00 AM – 4:00 PM (8 hrs)

Cozumel's reef was put on the international map by Jacques Cousteau in the early 1960s, and the diving community has never really left. The walls drop fast and visibility regularly hits 100 feet. For non-divers, the snorkeling at Palancar Gardens and Colombia Shallows runs shallow enough for young swimmers — a 7-year-old in fins can see the same fish and coral without going under. Expect parrotfish, sea turtles, and reef formations close to the surface.

San Miguel, the island's only town, has a waterfront malecón that's an easy walk after breakfast, taco stands open by 9:00 AM, and a market worth 20 minutes. Mr. Sanchos and Playa Mia are the two beach clubs where most families end up for the afternoon — both have pools, lounge chairs, fresh food, and enough activities to fill the time between arrival and the 4:00 PM all-aboard.
