Why Mo'orea

The Island, Honestly

— 30 minutes by high-speed ferry from the downtown dock. No domestic flight needed, no airport transfer on the far end. You step off the boat and you're there.

The lagoon here — Cook's Bay and Opunohu Bay flanking the island's jagged interior peaks — frames some of the best scenery in French Polynesia. The water surrounding Mo'orea offers excellent snorkeling throughout: rays, blacktip reef sharks, dense coral, and visibility that makes it easy to understand why divers keep coming back. The resorts are on the main island rather than outer motus, which means you can rent a scooter in the morning, circle the entire island, stop where you want, and be back for lunch. That freedom to move is something Bora Bora's motu-resort structure doesn't offer without a boat schedule.

A Note from Christina

Mo'orea is where I'd go for a honeymoon, and I want to be straight with you about why. I've sent couples to both islands, and what I keep hearing back from Bora Bora is that four or five nights in a motu resort is a long time if you're someone who likes to actually move around. The setting is extraordinary — don't get me wrong — but you're relying on a boat to get anywhere, and after a few days the resort can start to feel like a very beautiful box. Mo'orea's lagoon is just as good. The snorkeling is excellent all the way around the island — rays, blacktip reef sharks, coral at easy depth — and there's no shortage of places to get in the water. And because you're on the main island, you can grab a scooter on a Tuesday morning and just go — pineapple fields, mountain overlooks, a lunch spot you found on the road. That flexibility matters more on a longer stay than people expect.

That said, this is your honeymoon. If Bora Bora is the image you've had in your head, I will absolutely make it happen and make it great. I just want you going in with a clear picture of what both islands actually feel like to spend a week on — not just what they look like in photos.

One more thing — and I don't say this lightly. Your dates put you in Mo'orea during humpback whale season. August through October, these whales come to the warm waters of French Polynesia to give birth and nurse their calves. Mo'orea is one of a small handful of places in the world where you can actually get in the water with them. I've done it. It is one of the most extraordinary experiences of my life — a 40-ton animal, completely at ease, close enough that you feel the water move. There are no words that do it justice. If you go to Mo'orea and you do nothing else on this list, do the whale swim. The timing of your trip is not a coincidence — it's a reason.
